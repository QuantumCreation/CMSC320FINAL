Has the World Become a Better Place?

By Joey Hayden

INTRODUCTION

As the world entered the 21st century, technology and online media has slowly come to dominate our lives. Every day we are bombarded by news of wars, terrorism, domestic inequality, corruption, homocides, and political instability. However, many others, such as Steven Pinker have proposed that despite the bleak image that the media has painted, the world at large has in fact been improving over the last few centuries, and most drastically in the last few decades. This makes us ask, which view is correct? Has the world improved, despite the many horrors we regularly hear of in the news? Most of has have heard of the military buildup in China, the Russian takeover of Crimea and of the domestic political violence in the US. Despite these events and the many other problems we hear of so often, have the lives of the average human on Earth improved? Or have certain countries improved as others are left in the dust? Or perhaps, is the news correct and the world is headed in a downward spiral? Using a number of data sets from various 3rd party organizations, I will attempt to shed some light on whether or not the world at large has improved for the average citizen with various measures from economic stability and freedom to happiness, poverty, homocides rates and other statistical measures.

How Do You Measure Human Progress?

If we want to determine if the world has become a better place through data, we first need to figure out what we need to look for. I have decided a 6 different values, some of them are index's representing a number of different measures, while others are independent measurements. For example, the Economic Freedom Summary Index is created based on 12 different measures of economic freedom, whereas the Homocide rate is a single measurement. Below, I have listed each of the values I will be using for this analysis, along with what they represent.

List of Values Used to Judge Human Progress

  • Development Index
  • Homocide Rate
  • Hunger Index
  • Cantril Happiness Scale
  • Gender Pay Gap Comparison
  • Economic Freedom Index

Development Index

The Development Index uses life expectancy, access to education, and standard of living to generate an index value representing the total development of the culture, government, and average life of a citizen.

Homocide Rate

The Homocide Rate is the number of homocides per 100,000 individuals in the country. It is a rate, not a number, hence it scales with the size of the country in question.

Hunger Index

The Hunger Index measures how well the caloric and nutritional requirements are met in a specific country to generate a value. The difference between nutrition and caloric requirements are an important thing to note. An individual could have more than enough calories to survive, but lack necessary vitamins and minerals leading to sickness, disease, or even death in extreme cases.

Cantril Happiness Scale

The Cantril Happiness data is a scale where respondents are asked to rate their lives on a scale from 1-10, where 10 is the happiest, and 1 is the most depressed.

Gender Pay Gap Comparison

The Gender Pay Gap Comparison (Equality measure) is the percent difference between the wages of men and women in various countries over time.

Economic Freedom Index

The Economic Data has many different measures, but what I will be looking at is the Economic Freedom Summary Index, a measure of how free individuals are in a country to pursue economic oppurtunities. This considers 12 different measures, which are then broken into four categories. They are:

  • Rule of Law (property rights, government integrity, judicial effectiveness)
  • Government Size (government spending, tax burden, fiscal health)
  • Regulatory Efficiency (business freedom, labor freedom, monetary freedom)
  • Open Markets (trade freedom, investment freedom, financial freedom)

Again, these are in no way 100% comprehensive, but they do provide a very good clue as to whether or not the world at large is improving. Works such as Steven Pinker's "Enlightenment Now" cover a bit more ground in terms of the breadth of statistics and measures considered, though it is not quite as heavy in the data analysis itself. As a continuation of this, one could consider every index on the website "Our World In Data", where each index is a compilation of numerous measures. This would create a summary statistic that encompasses many dozen, if not hundreds of separate measures. That is beyond the scope of this introductory tutorial on data analysis, though it is an interesting idea to consider.

Great! Now we have cleaned up our data... or not. If we take a look at the table we printed out, there are still a number of spaces filled with "NaN". There are three ways we could deal with this. We could:

  • Remove rows and columns with missing data
  • Encode missing data
  • Impute missing data

Each of these has some pros and cons. For our case, we will be using the first option: Remove the missing data. However, the Economic Freedom Summary Index acts as a summary statistic for all of the other measures. This means that the organization collecting the data has already done some of their own imputation to create the index when certain measures in the index are missing, such as the case in in row 4052. There will still be missing data when even the index is missing, but it is an improvement nonetheless.

So, we've created some plots for each of the 6 measures we have decided to look at. However, they are rather messy, and it is difficult to draw any conclusions from what we have seen so far. The linear regressions we have created with the polyfit seems to indicate either no change, or a change for the better in most cases, but we can't say anything with certainty. This often happens when we are doing data analysis. Some type of plot or method may not give us the results we are looking for. However, the doesn't mean that we stop there. We continue to experiment and explore our data with different plots and other analysis methods. Looking at what data we have, we can see that timespan when data was collected differs, and the gap between each measurement also seems to change for different data sets. We also don't have a great idea of what a good value for these index's are. To get a more meaningful information out of this, I will break down and analyze each dataset individually.
